Why Anthropic Stood Up to the Pentagon

Dario and Daniela Amodei, in conversation with Oprah Winfrey

By Ben Sherry · Inc.

↓ or scroll to read at your own pace

THE INTERVIEW

A sit-down with Oprah

Oprah Winfrey sat down with Anthropic cofounders — and siblings — Dario and Daniela Amodei.

They opened up about why they refused demands from the U.S. military to remove the safety guardrails around Claude, their powerful AI model. And they shared the one major AI problem they haven't solved yet.

THE STAND

“Not worth defending”

Asked about the decision to stand up to the Department of Defense, Dario didn't hesitate.

It's not worth defending the country if we do things that go against the values of this country.

THE DEAL

The first AI in classified rooms

Anthropic began working with the Pentagon in 2024. Claude became the first AI model used by the military in classified environments.

But the original deal carried carveouts. Claude could not be used to:

  • Power lethal autonomy. No fully-autonomous weapons — Dario's example was a “drone army” commanded by one person.
  • Surveil citizens. No mass domestic surveillance on Americans.

THE PRESSURE

Designated a “supply chain risk”

Later, the Pentagon reopened negotiations to remove the carveouts — arguing they amounted to Anthropic making military policy over the American people and government.

Anthropic refused to budge. It was designated a supply chain risk — a label the company still holds today.

THE RECKONING

“This could be the end”

The Amodeis and their fellow cofounders met before deciding whether to acquiesce. They were united in saying no.

Both siblings told Oprah they truly believed this could have been the end of their company — yet it was still worth sticking to their convictions.

Daniela said they were ready for that hard choice because of three smaller decisions that “laddered up” to it.

DECISION ONE

No ads on Claude

The first choice: no advertising on Claude. Given the sensitive nature of the conversations people have with it, the founders felt they had to treat customer data with the utmost privacy and respect — making ads a non-starter.

Not everyone agreed. There were “quiet forces,” Daniela said — inside the company and among investors — who saw ads as necessary revenue, pointing to Google and Facebook.

Sorry, that's just not how we're going to do things. We'll find another way.

DECISION TWO

The bill voted down 99 to 1

A bill in Congress would have banned all state regulation of AI — without adding any federal regulation either.

People warned Dario: “You'll break all your political relationships. All the other companies will hate you if you speak out.”

He spoke out anyway — an op-ed in The New York Times, “Don't Let AI Companies off the Hook.”

That bill got voted down in the Senate 99 to one.

DECISION THREE

Backing real regulation

Anthropic supported California's SB 1047 — a bill Daniela called the first attempt to create a regulatory framework around the very risks Anthropic had been naming since day one.

Not a perfect bill, she said — but “on balance, we think it's better that these regulations exist… so that AI doesn't hurt people.”

Angry calls followed — from former colleagues, investors, and rival tech leaders. The bill passed the legislature, then was vetoed by Governor Gavin Newsom.

THE LESSON

Sometimes it's worth the risk

Taken together, the Amodeis said, these three decisions helped Anthropic's leadership find their hardline stances — and taught them one lesson.

Advocating for a policy — especially one unpopular among peers — is risky. But sometimes, it can be worth the risk.

THE UNSOLVED

The problem they can't crack

There's one thing the Amodeis admit they haven't figured out: how to give everyday people a voice at the table when it comes to AI.

Oprah said she's heard from many who feel powerless — like passengers on a train that's already left the station.

THE ADMISSION

Nobody has figured it out

Dario agreed with Oprah's worry.

We need to find some way for everyone to be an active participant in what is happening.

But he added the uncomfortable truth: nobody — not even Anthropic — has figured that part out yet.

THE FUTURE

It won't happen on its own

Still, Dario told Oprah, a better future is possible with AI.

But it's not going to happen on its own. It's up to us — the companies, the people, the governments — to work together to bring that future into reality.

THE PORTRAIT

Two siblings, one company

What Oprah drew out, more than a news story, was a portrait of two people running a company by conviction.

  • Family. A brother and sister, plus their cofounders, united in the hard calls.
  • Pattern. No ads. An op-ed. A California bill. Then the Pentagon.
  • Honesty. Willing to say what they still don't know.
  • Stakes. A company they'd risk losing to keep their word.
