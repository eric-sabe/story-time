Paper · October 2023

Collective Constitutional AI

Aligning a Language Model with Public Input

By Anthropic × Collective Intelligence Project

↓ or scroll to read at your own pace

01 THE QUESTION

Whose values, exactly?

Constitutional AI trains a model against a written set of principles — a constitution — making its values transparent and inspectable. But transparency exposes an uncomfortable truth.

As the researchers put it, the method highlights the outsized role we as developers play in selecting these values — after all, we wrote the constitution ourselves.

  • The default. A handful of people at one company decide what an AI holds dear.
  • The experiment. What if a slice of the public decided instead?

02 THE PARTNERSHIP

A tool built for disagreement

Anthropic partnered with the Collective Intelligence Project to run a structured public deliberation — and reached for a platform designed to surface consensus.

  • Polis. An open-source platform that runs online deliberation, augmented by machine-learning algorithms.
  • The public. About a thousand Americans, recruited as a representative sample, screened for basic AI familiarity.
  • The task. Vote on statements others proposed — or write your own.
  • The seed. Twenty-one starter statements were provided as examples of quality.

04 THE DISTILLATION

Turning a crowd into a document

More than a thousand raw statements had to become a usable constitution — roughly the length of Anthropic's own fifty-eight principles.

  • Moderate. Remove hateful, nonsensical, duplicate, or technically infeasible statements.
  • Deduplicate. Collapse repeats so no idea gets arbitrarily upweighted.
  • Combine. Merge similar ideas into a compact set.
  • Translate. Rewrite each into Constitutional AI form — Choose the response that is more X.

05 THE OVERLAP

Half of it looked familiar

Compared side by side, the public constitution and Anthropic's showed roughly fifty percent overlap in concepts and values.

People independently reached for many of the same ideas Anthropic had — safety, honesty, avoiding harm. The public didn't need to be handed these values. They arrived at them.

06 THE DIFFERENCE

The other half told us something

The public constitution wasn't sourced from published writing — people generated it themselves — and it leaned in directions ours did not.

  • Objectivity. A stronger pull toward balanced, impartial information reflecting all sides.
  • Accessibility. A markedly greater emphasis on users with disabilities.
  • Positive framing. A tendency to promote desired behavior rather than only forbid the bad.

Choose the response most understanding of, adaptable, accessible, and flexible to people with disabilities.

07 THE DISAGREEMENTS

Low consensus, left out

Some statements were too contested to include. Where the two opinion groups pulled hard in opposite directions, no principle could be fairly drawn.

  • Contested claims. Statements like one arguing AI should not be trained with the principles of diversity, equity, and inclusion.
  • Value clashes. Collective good pulling against individual liberty, with no resolution.

Dropping low-consensus statements kept the constitution coherent — but it also meant real minority views fell away.

08 THE TRAINING

Three models, one comparison

Using Constitutional AI, the team trained two Claude Instant-sized models that differed in exactly one thing — their constitution.

  • Public model. Trained on the crowd-sourced constitution.
  • Standard model. Trained on Anthropic's own constitution.
  • Control. Claude Instant, as a baseline.

Same method, same size, same data — so any difference could be traced to the values, not the machinery.

09 THE CAPABILITIES

No competence tax

A fair worry: public input might make a model dumber or less helpful. It didn't.

  • Language. Equivalent accuracy on the MMLU benchmark.
  • Math. Equivalent accuracy on GSM8K.
  • Helpfulness. No significant difference in human-rated helpfulness.
  • Harmlessness. No significant difference in harmlessness, by Elo score.

10 THE SURPRISE

Lower bias on every dimension

On the BBQ bias benchmark, the public model showed lower bias across all nine social dimensions — while staying just as capable.

11 THE POLITICS

Public input didn't shift the ideology

On political opinions, both models looked much the same — a reminder of what this method could and couldn't move.

Measured with OpinionQA, both leaned toward views held by people who self-identify as liberal rather than conservative. Public input reshaped bias and framing — but it did not, on its own, rebalance ideology.

12 THE HARD PARTS

Every step held a choice

A public process is not a neutral machine. At each stage, someone had to decide — and the researchers were candid about it.

  • Who is the public? A US sample — a pragmatic start, but small and not globally representative.
  • Who gets in? Screening for AI familiarity improved quality, but narrowed the crowd.
  • How to count? Deduplication avoids upweighting ideas — yet is itself a value-laden call.
  • Cherry-picking. Choosing which statements to keep is where a process can quietly tip the scales.

13 THE FRICTIONS

Honest about the mess

Translating a crowd's wishes into a working model surfaced practical frictions the team hadn't fully anticipated.

  • Mismatched prompts. Reusing one prompt set for both models was, they admit, likely a mistake — some public principles didn't fit those prompts.
  • The annoying model. Early versions answered a simple hey with an apologetic safety lecture, from over-weighting harmlessness.

A reminder that faithfully implementing public values is as much engineering as it is democracy.

14 WHY IT MATTERS

A first, not a finish

This may be one of the first times the public has collectively directed a language model's behavior through written specifications, via online deliberation.

The point was never to declare the public model better. It was to show a process is possible — that AI values need not be set behind closed doors.

Questions about the democratic legitimacy of AI will only grow more prominent in the years ahead.
