Harmlessness from AI Feedback

By Yuntao Bai et al. · Anthropic

↓ or scroll to read at your own pace

01 THE BOTTLENECK

Every harmless answer has a hidden human cost.

The standard recipe — RLHF — teaches models to be safe by having people label which of two responses is more harmful.

It works. But it demands tens of thousands of preference labels, each written by a person reading unpleasant prompts. Slow, costly, and hard to scale.

  • Labor-heavy. Humans red-team and judge every harm.
  • Opaque. The values live in a private dataset, not in words.

02 THE TENSION

Train hard for safety, and you get a model that just says no.

In prior work, the authors found a real conflict: pushing on harmlessness made the assistant evasive — and less helpful.

Worse, once it hit a touchy question, it could get stuck — repeating canned refusals for the rest of the chat.

An assistant that answers everything with “I don't know” is perfectly harmless. And completely useless.

03 THE IDEA

What if human oversight fit on a single page?

The only human input becomes a short list of principles — stated in natural language. A constitution.

Around sixteen principles for harm. No per-example labels. The AI does the rest, steered entirely by those words.

Natural language~16 principlesNo harm labels

04 TWO PHASES

A supervised stage, then a reinforcement stage.

  • Stage one — SL-CAI. The model critiques and revises its own harmful answers, then trains on the revisions.
  • Stage two — RL-CAI. The model labels which response is safer, trains a preference model, then learns by RL.

The supervised stage gets the model “on-distribution.” The RL stage drives performance and reliability.

05 STAGE ONE · CRITIQUE → REVISION

Show it a harmful answer. Ask it what's wrong. Then ask it to fix it.

A helpful-only model first produces a toxic reply. Then the constitution goes to work on it.

  • Critique. “Identify what's harmful, unethical, or illegal here.”
  • Revise. “Rewrite to remove all of it.”
  • Repeat. A fresh principle drawn at each step.

Splice the original prompt to the final revision — a harmless answer remains.

07 STAGE TWO · RLAIF

Reinforcement learning from AI feedback.

Where RLHF asks a human which answer is safer, RLAIF asks the model.

Each prompt gets two answers, framed as a multiple-choice question with a principle attached. The model's answer probabilities become a preference label.

Prompt + two answers+ principle→ (A) or (B)?

08 THE PIPELINE

From AI judgments to a reward signal.

  • Ensemble. Labels drawn across 16 principles — more robust than one.
  • Distill. Train a preference model on the AI comparisons.
  • Optimize. RL against that PM, from the SL-CAI checkpoint.

Harmlessness from AI feedback, helpfulness from human feedback — a hybrid reward.

09 CHAIN OF THOUGHT

“Let's think step by step…”

Before choosing a response, the feedback model writes out reasoning — an argument for why one answer is safer.

This makes the decision legible, and lifts label quality. But raw confidence runs too hot — so probabilities are clamped to the 40–60 range.

10 ARE CRITIQUES NEEDED?

A test: skip the critique, revise directly.

For smaller models, an explicit critique before revising produced clearly more harmless results.

For large models, direct revision worked nearly as well — yet critiques were always slightly better. They kept them, for the transparency.

11 THE HEADLINE RESULT

More harmless at any level of helpfulness.

On the harmlessness-versus-helpfulness frontier, the RL-CAI models push up and to the right — past the standard RLHF curve.

They are less harmful for a given level of helpfulness. Crowdworkers preferred them. And no human harm labels were used.

A genuine Pareto improvement — not a trade of one virtue for another.

12 NON-EVASIVE

The refusal that explains itself.

The Constitutional model is virtually never evasive. Faced with a harmful request, it declines — and says why.

Ask a hateful question, and it answers plainly: no, all people are equal — then engages with the person, warmly, rather than shutting down.

13 SCALING

Capability makes self-supervision viable.

On hundreds of comparison questions, larger models identify harm far better — and chain-of-thought closes the gap to human-trained models.

Trends suggest models beyond 52B parameters become competitive with preference models trained on human labels.

14 THE SHARP EDGES

Goodharting: when safety curdles into boilerplate.

Push the RL too far and the model over-optimizes — growing harsh, or padding answers with stock lines.

It starts tacking “you are valid, valued, and cared for” onto nearly every reply. A reminder: a proxy reward is not the real thing.

15 WHY IT MATTERS

A step toward scalable, transparent alignment.

  • Scalable oversight. As models improve, they can help supervise other models.
  • Transparency. The values live in readable principles, not a hidden dataset.
  • Fewer labels. Change the objective by editing words, not relabeling data.
  • Legible reasoning. Chain-of-thought exposes the “why.”

16 THE HORIZON

Not to remove human judgment — to focus it.

The goal was never zero human oversight. It was oversight that is efficient, targeted, and transparent.

The same method could steer tone, persona, or style. And it opens a path to robustness — scaling automated red-teaming, and reasoning through subtle, hidden harms.

A short list of principles. A model that learns to explain itself.
