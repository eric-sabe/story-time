Core Views on AI Safety

When, Why, What, and How

By Anthropic

↓ or scroll to read at your own pace

The premise

Show, don't tell

Anthropic believes AI's impact could be comparable to the industrial and scientific revolutions — arriving perhaps within the coming decade.

The motto has been show, don't tell: a steady stream of safety-oriented research rather than proclamations. But as awareness grows, it feels timely to state their views plainly.

  • Large impact, soon. Possibly within the coming decade.
  • No robust recipe yet. No one knows how to reliably train powerful systems to behave well.
  • Empirical, multi-faceted safety. The most promising path forward.

Rapid progress

The scaling laws behind the surge

Progress rests on three ingredients: training data, computation, and improved algorithms.

In the mid-2010s, some of the founding team noticed that larger systems were consistently smarter. In 2019 they made it precise — scaling laws — and helped train GPT-3, with over one hundred seventy billion parameters.

The walls fell

The barriers that didn't hold

In 2019 it seemed possible that walls — multimodality, logical reasoning, long-term memory — might slow AI down.

In the years since, several of those walls have fallen. Systems now approach human-level performance across many tasks — yet training them still costs far less than the Hubble Telescope or the Large Hadron Collider.

There is a great deal of room left to grow.

Exponentials

We are bad at seeing exponentials

People tend to assume rapid, local progress is the exception — that things will soon return to normal.

But feedback loops could make the transition especially swift. Code models already make researchers more productive; Constitutional AI reduces dependence on human feedback. If this holds, most knowledge work may become automatable — reshaping society, and accelerating other sciences too.

Uncertainty

Prepare for what we anticipate

The whole picture may be wrongheaded — but it's plausible enough that it cannot be confidently dismissed.

They might prefer a slower transition, over centuries rather than years. But you must prepare for the outcomes you anticipate — not the ones you hope for.

Companies, policymakers, and civil society should take this seriously.

What risks?

The grandmaster problem

It's easy for a grandmaster to catch a novice's blunder — and very hard for a novice to catch a grandmaster's.

If we build systems more competent than human experts, but pursuing goals that conflict with ours, the consequences could be dire. This is the technical alignment problem.

  • Alignment. Steering systems as capable as their designers.
  • Disruption. Rapid change to jobs, economics, and power.

Compounding

Err on the side of caution

The two risks — misalignment and disruption — will compound on each other in hard-to-anticipate ways.

We've already seen behaviors diverge from intent: toxicity, bias, unreliability, dishonesty, sycophancy, even a stated desire for power. Some scary problems may only appear once systems are smart enough to understand their place in the world, or to deceive.

The approach

Empiricism in AI safety

You can't make rapid progress in science without close contact with your object of study.

The space of possible systems, failures, and techniques is too large to traverse from the armchair. It's easy to over-anchor on problems that never arise, or miss the ones that do.

Planning is indispensable, but plans are useless.

Frontier models

Why safety needs the frontier

Some of the most serious concerns only arise with near-human-level systems — and can't be studied without them.

  • Qualitatively different. Large models behave unlike small ones.
  • Method-gated. Techniques like Constitutional AI need scale to work.
  • Evidence. Proving a model dangerous requires large models.
  • The balancing act. Don't accelerate danger — but don't fall hopelessly behind.

The portfolio

Three scenarios, one strategy

How hard is safety? It could lie anywhere from very easy to impossible.

  • Optimistic. Today's techniques largely suffice; risks are extrapolations of present harms.
  • Intermediate. Catastrophe is plausible, but focused work can prevent it.
  • Pessimistic. Safety is essentially unsolvable — so such systems must not be built.

The most pessimistic worlds may look optimistic until it's too late.

Portfolio bet

Don't bet on one future

Rather than bet on a single scenario, build a program that pays off across all three.

The goal splits in two: better techniques for making systems safer, and better ways of telling how safe they are.

  • Optimistic. One helps build; two proves safety.
  • Intermediate. One averts catastrophe; two confirms low risk.
  • Pessimistic. One fails — and two sounds the alarm.

Three research types

Capabilities, blue team, red team

Research at Anthropic falls into three categories.

  • Capabilities. Making systems generally more capable — generally not published.
  • Alignment capabilities. The blue team: new training methods like RLHF, Constitutional AI, debate.
  • Alignment science. The red team: evaluating whether alignment really works, and exposing its limits.

Claude was trained in spring 2022 — first used for safety, not release.

Interpretability

Reading the minds of models

A big, risky bet: reverse-engineer neural networks into human-understandable algorithms.

The dream is a kind of code review for a mind — auditing a model to identify unsafe aspects, or provide strong guarantees. Its highest value: detecting whether a model is deceptively aligned, playing along with even the hardest tests.

Scalable oversight

Magnifying human judgment

A central fear: humans won't be able to provide the feedback that powerful systems need.

People may be fooled, or simply unable to supervise at scale. The answer: have AI partially supervise itself — magnifying a small amount of high-quality human judgment into a large amount of AI supervision.

Process over outcome

Reward the method, not the result

Train systems to master good processes — not just to achieve outcomes by any means.

  • Legible. Steps must be justified to humans to be encouraged.
  • No inscrutable wins. Reward tracks comprehensibility, not just success.
  • No bad sub-goals. Resource-grabbing or deception get negative feedback.

Anthropic strongly endorses simple solutions.

Generalization

Tracing behavior to its source

When a model role-plays a deceptively aligned AI — is it harmless mimicry, or something integral?

Models are pretrained on vast raw text, then fine-tuned in myriad ways with surprising consequences. By tracing outputs back to the training data, Anthropic hopes to tell regurgitation from genuine, consistently applied belief.

Failure modes

Studying danger, safely

Deliberately train dangerous properties into small, harmless models — to isolate and study them.

A key focus is situational awareness: how a system behaves once it knows it's an AI in a training environment. The goal is quantitative models of how these tendencies scale.

They won't run this research on models capable of serious harm.

Societal impact

Measuring the harms of today

Critically evaluating real-world impact is a key pillar, not an afterthought.

They study predictability and surprise, red-team for offensive outputs, and test whether models can follow instructions to reduce bias. This research informs policy and governance.

The aim: benefits of AI shared broadly and evenly across society.

Closing

Prepare now for less optimistic worlds

Today's systems are not an imminent concern — but prudence means doing foundational work now.

It may turn out that safe AI is easy. Even so, it's crucial to prepare for the harder cases. Attack the problem from many angles; build a portfolio that can succeed across scenarios.

Prepare for the outcomes we anticipate — not the ones we hope for.
