Essay · January 2025

On DeepSeek and Export Controls

What DeepSeek really meant, and why chips are the chokepoint

By Dario Amodei · Anthropic

↓ or scroll to read at your own pace

01 THE MOMENT

A panic built on a misreading

DeepSeek's release triggered a wave of alarm — and a headline number that didn't mean what people thought it meant.

The claim rippling through markets was that a Chinese lab had reached the frontier for roughly six million dollars, a fraction of what American companies spend. If true, it would suggest export controls were pointless and the U.S. lead was gone.

Dario's argument is that the panic inverted the real lesson. Understanding why requires understanding how AI development actually behaves.

02 DYNAMIC ONE

Scaling laws

Spend more on training, and models get predictably, reliably smarter across the board.

This isn't a hunch — it's one of the most robust patterns in the field. More compute, more data, more scale yields better performance on nearly every cognitive task.

  • A million-dollar model might solve twenty percent of coding tasks.
  • A hundred-million-dollar model might solve sixty percent.

The gap between those two is the difference between an undergraduate and a PhD.

03 DYNAMIC TWO

Shifting the curve

Better ideas make the same result cheaper — they shift the whole cost curve down.

Dario calls these compute multipliers. A clever architecture or efficiency trick lets you reach a given capability for less. Small ones are common; large ones are rare.

04 DYNAMIC THREE

A new paradigm

Occasionally the method itself changes — a new place to pour scale, early on its own curve.

From 2020 to 2023, the game was pretrained models. In 2024, a second stage arrived: reinforcement learning to train chains of reasoning.

Because this paradigm is young, small amounts of spending still buy large gains — for now.

05 THE MODELS

What DeepSeek actually shipped

Two releases — and neither one was a break in the trend.

  • V3, December 2024. A strong pretrained model, close to top U.S. models on some tasks, trained for notably less.
  • R1, January 2025. V3 plus the new reasoning stage — essentially replicating what OpenAI did with o1.

V3 is the genuine innovation. R1 caught the headlines. But R1 was likely very cheap to make from V3, because reasoning is early on its curve.

06 THE NUMBER

About that six million dollars

The famous figure is one training run — not the cost of the company.

Six million dollars covers compute for a single model. It excludes research, prior experiments, salaries, and the hardware behind them all.

  • Claude 3.5 Sonnet cost a few tens of millions to train.
  • It was trained nine to twelve months earlier — and stayed ahead on many evaluations.

Comparing a headline training cost to a whole company's R&D is comparing different things.

07 ON TREND

Right where the curve predicts

Run the math on the shifting curve, and V3's cost is totally, boringly normal.

Costs for a given capability fall over time — inference prices for GPT-4-level quality dropped roughly tenfold in a year and a half.

V3 sits about a factor of two behind the current frontier on the scaling curve. Being roughly eight times cheaper to train would be exactly on trend.

Not ahead of the curve. On it.

08 THE CHIPS

Not a shoestring operation

DeepSeek reportedly ran on tens of thousands of advanced chips — not a garage budget.

That's within a factor of two to three of what major U.S. labs command. DeepSeek's edge was engineering talent, deployed against real resources — not scarcity overcome by magic.

09 THE PARADOX

Cheaper means we buy more

Efficiency doesn't shrink compute demand. It grows it.

When training gets cheaper, no one pockets the savings and stops. The prize at the end of the curve is so valuable that everyone simply traverses the curve faster — spending the savings on smarter models.

This is the old Jevons pattern: greater efficiency, greater total consumption. Budgets keep climbing toward billions.

10 THE REVERSAL

Why controls matter more

If efficiency raises total chip demand, then who gets the chips still decides everything.

The naive takeaway was: DeepSeek proves chips don't matter, so drop the controls. The actual logic runs the other way.

Because the world will keep pouring millions of chips into ever-larger training, control over that supply becomes more decisive, not less. DeepSeek strengthens the case.

11 THE STAKES

Two possible worlds

By 2026 or 2027, powerful AI will need millions of chips and tens of billions of dollars. Who has them shapes everything.

  • Unipolar. Controls hold. Democracies keep the lead — and because AI helps build better AI, a temporary edge could become durable.
  • Bipolar. China reaches parity. It could pour more talent and capital into military uses, and take a commanding global lead.

12 THE JUDGMENT

Why one led by democracies is safer

Dario's aim isn't to deny China the benefits of AI — it's to prevent authoritarian military dominance.

He's clear the DeepSeek researchers seem like smart, curious people. But they answer to an authoritarian government.

A world where the most powerful technology sits with democracies is, in his view, the safer bet — not a perfect one, but the one worth defending.

13 THE EVIDENCE

The controls are working

DeepSeek's chip mix isn't a failure of controls — it's a snapshot of them taking hold.

  • H100s. Banned — likely smuggled.
  • H800s. Shipped before the October ban closed that gap.
  • H20s. Still allowed — a loophole Dario thinks should close.

A full fleet of top-line H100s would signal failure. This mix signals loopholes closing, one by one.

14 THE SCALE TEST

You can hide a billion, not a hundred

The controls don't need to be perfect. They need to make massive buildouts impossible to conceal.

A billion dollars of chips can be smuggled and hidden. A hundred billion cannot. Millions of chips are physically hard to sneak across borders.

The frontier of the future is a giant, visible thing. Controls raise the cost of matching it — and that margin is the whole point.

15 THE STAKES

One of our most powerful tools

Export controls are, in Dario's view, one of the most powerful levers we have over how powerful AI arrives.

DeepSeek didn't weaken that case. It clarified it. Progress is smooth and predictable; efficiency raises demand; chips are the chokepoint.

The question isn't whether China gets AI's benefits. It's whether a temporary lead becomes a durable, democratic one — before the window closes.
