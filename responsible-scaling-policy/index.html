The Responsible Scaling Policy

AI Safety Levels, and a commitment to stop

By Anthropic

↓ or scroll to read at your own pace

01 The problem

A promise you can check

The Responsible Scaling Policy targets a specific fear: catastrophic risk — an AI model that directly causes large-scale devastation.

Not everyday harms, but the rare, severe cases — either deliberate misuse by bad actors, or a model acting harmfully against its designers' intent.

  • Misuse. A model helping a state or a terrorist build a weapon.
  • Autonomy. A model taking harmful action on its own.

02 The borrowed idea

Learning from the biosafety labs

The framework is modeled loosely on the US government's Biosafety Levels for handling dangerous biological materials.

A lab studying a common cold needs modest precautions. A lab handling a lethal pathogen needs airlocks, sealed suits, and strict protocol. The safeguards scale to the danger.

Same logic, aimed at AI: AI Safety Levels.

03 The ladder

Four rungs, rising with the risk

AI Safety Levels run from ASL-1 to ASL-4 and beyond — each rung demanding a stricter demonstration of safety than the last.

ASL-1ASL-2ASL-3ASL-4+

The rule binding them is the same all the way up: safety, security, and operational standards appropriate to a model's potential for catastrophic risk.

04 ASL-1

The bottom rung: no real danger

ASL-1 covers systems that pose no meaningful catastrophic risk at all.

Think of a chess-playing program, or a language model from 2018. Powerful in narrow ways, but nobody's threat model. A baseline, for reference.

05 ASL-2

Where today's models live

ASL-2 covers systems that show early signs of dangerous capabilities — but not yet in a reliably useful way.

A model might, for instance, give instructions related to a bioweapon. But the information is unreliable, and no better than what a determined person could already find with a search engine.

Anthropic's current Claude models are classified ASL-2.

06 ASL-3

The line that changes everything

ASL-3 is where a model substantially increases the risk of catastrophic misuse compared to a world without it — or shows low-level autonomy.

  • Real uplift. Meaningfully helping someone toward a bio or cyber weapon, beyond non-AI tools.
  • Early autonomy. The first flickers of a model that can act on its own.

07 ASL-4+

The rungs not yet built

ASL-4 and beyond are deliberately left undefined — anticipated, but not yet specified.

They would involve qualitative escalations in misuse potential and autonomy — dangers speculative enough that pinning them down now would be guesswork. The policy commits to defining them later — but not too late.

08 The mechanism

If-then, all the way up

The engine of the whole policy is a conditional: if a model reaches a capability, the matching safeguards must already be in place.

If a model shows ASL-3 capabilities, then ASL-3 safety and security measures must be ready — or the model cannot proceed. Capability triggers obligation.

09 The commitment

The willingness to stop

If capabilities outrun the safeguards, the policy implicitly requires a pause — a temporary halt to training more powerful models.

It's the rare corporate promise that can bite its author. Fall behind on safety, and you don't get to keep scaling. You wait until you catch up.

A pause is not the failure of the plan. It is the plan working.

10 Evaluations

How you know you've crossed a line

The whole system depends on knowing what a model can actually do — which means testing it hard.

For ASL-3, the bar is stark: don't deploy a model if it shows any meaningful catastrophic misuse risk under adversarial testing by world-class red-teamers. Not just running the tests — passing them.

11 Define it first

Writing the next rule before you need it

The promise about that fog above the ladder: define ASL-4 before reaching ASL-3.

Always keep one rung of safety defined ahead of where the models actually are. Some of it may require breakthroughs not yet in hand — like using interpretability to show, mechanistically, that a model won't misbehave.

12 Accountability

Not just taking their word for it

A self-imposed rule needs structure to keep it honest — so the policy builds in oversight.

  • Board-approved. Changes require board sign-off, after consulting the Long-Term Benefit Trust.
  • Outside expertise. Built with help from external evaluators like ARC Evals.
  • An early draft. Explicitly a first iteration, to be revised.

13 Why it matters

A race to the top

The hope isn't only to make Anthropic safer — it's to change what competition rewards.

A concrete, checkable commitment can be copied. It offers inspiration to other labs, to nonprofits, and to policymakers — a working template for what regulation might one day require.

Turn the race away from cutting corners — and toward solving the safety problems first.

14 The wager

Business as usual, until it can't be

For now, the policy changes nothing you'd notice — no product disrupted, no availability lost.

It works like pre-market testing in aviation or cars: invisible when all is well, decisive when it isn't. A promise waiting for the day it's needed.

The measure of a safety commitment is whether it can ever cost you something.
