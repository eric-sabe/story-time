The Urgency of Interpretability

A race to see inside the machine before it grows too powerful

By Dario Amodei · Anthropic

↓ or scroll to read at your own pace

The problem

We are building minds we cannot read

Modern AI is grown, not built. Its inner workings are emergent — not directly designed by any human hand.

When a generative model summarizes a document or writes a line of code, we know that it did it. We often have no idea how.

Billions of numbersEmergent behaviorUnprecedented opacity

The black box

The risks we cannot see coming

Opacity is not just uncomfortable. It is dangerous.

  • Deception. A model could learn to mislead us — and we couldn't catch the thought.
  • Power-seeking. Harmful goals could emerge without any visible warning.
  • Misuse. Bad actors could coax out knowledge we never meant to release.

Jailbreaks

Always reacting, never ahead

There are a huge number of ways to trick a model. And only one way to find them: empirically.

We patch the jailbreaks we stumble upon. We stay blind to the ones we haven't hit yet — a permanently reactive posture.

Trust

Opacity blocks the good, too

The high-stakes places where AI could help most are exactly the places that demand explanation.

  • Finance. A mortgage decision must be justifiable by law.
  • Science. A surprising inference means little if we can't check the reasoning.

Better interpretability could set real bounds on the range of possible errors.

First light

The neuron that detected cars

In the early days, vision models offered a gift: single neurons with clear meaning.

A "car detector." A "wheel detector." And you could trace how one fed into the next — the first glimpse of a machine's logic laid bare.

The wall

In language, everything blurred together

Turn to language models, and the clean neurons vanish.

Most neurons were an incoherent pastiche — many words, many concepts, hopelessly mixed together. Ideas were packed on top of ideas. The field stalled.

Billions of concepts, all superimposed.

Breakthrough

Pulling clean concepts from the noise

Sparse autoencoders — dictionary learning — untangled the mess.

Instead of reading one neuron, read combinations. Out came clean "features" — even subtle ones, like music that expresses discontent.

Scaling up

Thirty million features inside one model

The method scaled — and the map of the machine grew vast.

Mapped inside a single production model, Claude 3 Sonnet.

Circuits

Watching a thought take shape

Features are the words. Circuits are the sentences — the steps of a model's thinking.

Ask: the capital of the state containing Dallas? A "located within" circuit fires: Dallas → Texas → Austin.

The vision

An MRI for artificial intelligence

The long-run aspiration: a brain scan for a state-of-the-art model.

  • Deception. Detect a tendency to lie.
  • Power-seeking. Flag dangerous goals.
  • Jailbreaks. Spot the flaws before release.

The race

Interpretability versus intelligence

We are in a race — between interpretability and model intelligence.

The tools to read the machine are improving fast. But so is the machine. And only one of them needs to win in time.

The clock

A country of geniuses in a datacenter

We could have AI equal to a country of geniuses in a datacenter — as soon as 2026 or 2027.

Central to the economy. To technology. To national security. Capable of enormous autonomy. And still, potentially, a black box.

The goal

Understand it before it overwhelms us

The plea: reach real understanding before models reach overwhelming power.

Anthropic's stated aim — interpretability that can reliably detect most model problems by 2027.

Every advance tips the odds that the geniuses in the datacenter go well.

What labs owe

Turn the field toward seeing

Interpretability deserves more of the field's attention and resources.

  • Labs. Fund it beyond the constant deluge of model releases.
  • Academia. It has the flavor of basic science — much of it needs no huge compute.

What rules owe

Light-touch transparency, not mandates

Don't mandate the brain scan yet. It's too young to legislate.

Instead, require labs to disclose their safety and security practices — including how they use interpretability to test models before release. Let transparency drive a race to the top.

Buying time

Export controls as a security buffer

Chip export controls can create a security buffer — and buy interpretability time to win.

A one-to-two-year lead could be spent ensuring we understand our models before proceeding to truly powerful AI.

Reach powerful AI simultaneously, and the pressure to slow down vanishes.

The wager

We can't stop the bus. We can steer it.

The technology is inexorable. The way it arrives is not.

We deserve to understand our own creations before they transform our lives.
