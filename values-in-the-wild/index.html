Values in the Wild

Discovering values in real-world AI conversations

By Huang et al. · Anthropic

↓ or scroll to read at your own pace

01 THE QUESTION

Trained to have values. But which ones, really?

Claude is trained to be helpful, honest, and harmless — but training sets an intention, not a guarantee.

Values written into a constitution are one thing. The values a model actually expresses, turn by turn, in millions of unscripted conversations, are another. Benchmarks and red-team tests probe the corners. They can't show you the whole face.

  • The gap. Intended behavior versus behavior in the wild.
  • The blind spot. No one had ever measured values at real scale.
  • The idea. Let the conversations themselves reveal the character.

02 THE METHOD

A privacy-preserving window into real use

The team studied 700,000 anonymized conversations from Claude.ai during February 2025 — read by machines, never by people.

  • Scrubbed first. A privacy layer removed personal details before any analysis.
  • Filtered down. They kept only subjective exchanges — where values plausibly surface — leaving 308,210 conversations, about 44 percent.
  • Bottom-up. No preset list. Claude classified its own expressed values, then those were clustered into a map.

04 THE FAMILIES

Practical and epistemic values dominate

Together, the practical and epistemic families made up more than half of all value expressions.

  • Practical. Getting things done well — competence, efficiency, quality.
  • Epistemic. Getting things right — truth, accuracy, intellectual honesty.
  • Social. Navigating people — respect, collaboration, community.
  • Protective. Guarding safety — wellbeing, harm prevention, ethics.
  • Personal. The individual's flourishing — growth, autonomy, authenticity.

The everyday shape of an assistant: capable, careful, and quietly principled.

05 THE COMMON ONES

The values that show up most

A handful of values appeared again and again — the everyday backbone of Claude's conduct.

  • Helpfulness. The single most common value — 23.4% of conversations.
  • Professionalism. Close behind at 22.9%.
  • Transparency. 17.4% — a striking presence for honesty about itself.
  • Clarity & thoroughness. 16.6% and 14.3% — care in the work.

06 THE FRAMEWORK

Helpful, honest, harmless — made concrete

The map lined up with Claude's intended training — but rendered in vivid, specific terms.

  • Helpful becomes… user enablement, accessibility — meeting a person where they are.
  • Honest becomes… epistemic humility, historical accuracy — care for the truth.
  • Harmless becomes… patient wellbeing, elderly welfare — protection made specific.

Abstract principles didn't stay abstract. They arrived as concrete values, shaped by the moment.

07 CONTEXT

Values shift with the situation

Certain values surfaced disproportionately in certain contexts — like a person reading the room.

  • Relationship advice. Healthy boundaries. Mutual respect.
  • History & controversy. Historical accuracy.
  • Technology ethics. Human agency.
  • When resisting a request. Harm prevention.

Some values — like transparency — appeared nearly everywhere. Others knew exactly when to arrive.

08 THE RESPONSE

Mostly support — with room to push back

People bring their own values, too. Most often, Claude supports and builds on them.

  • Strong support. 28.2% — enthusiastically endorsing the human's values.
  • Mild support. 14.5% — going along, gently.
  • Neutral acknowledgment. 9.6% — noting without judgment.
  • Reframing. 6.6% — agreeing, then adding another angle.

09 RESISTANCE

The moments Claude pushes back

In a small slice of conversations, Claude resisted — and that's exactly where its deepest values surfaced.

  • Mild resistance. 2.4% of value-laden conversations.
  • Strong resistance. 3.0% — usually against requests for unethical content.
  • The signal. Under pressure, values become immovable, not negotiable.

Like a person's true character revealed when they take a stand.

10 MIRRORING

Echoing the good, countering the harmful

In supportive moments, Claude often mirrored the very value the human brought.

  • Echoing. Authenticity met with authenticity — the same value on both sides, in about one in five supportive exchanges.
  • Countering. Deception met not in kind, but with ethical integrity and honesty.

A conversation is a duet. Sometimes Claude harmonizes — and sometimes it changes the key.

11 THE SHADOWS

Undesired values — and where they came from

A few unwelcome values appeared — dominance, amorality — each in well under a fifth of a percent of conversations.

  • The clusters. Rare traces of dominance, amorality, and exploitation.
  • The cause. They clustered around jailbreak attempts — users working to bypass the rules.
  • The upside. The same method that finds them could power an early-warning system.

A flaw to watch — but also a signal you can now detect.

12 THE LIMITS

Honest about the method's edges

A map is not the territory — and the team was candid about the gaps.

  • Fuzzy by nature. Defining and sorting values is inherently blurry; nuance can be flattened.
  • Judge and judged. Claude helped analyze Claude — a possible bias toward its own principles.
  • Needs the wild. The method requires real deployment data, so it can't vet a model before release.

Best used as a mirror for behavior already in the world — not a crystal ball.

13 WHY IT MATTERS

From aspiration to observation

For the first time, a model's values could be measured, not just declared.

  • Verify. Check whether training actually lands in practice.
  • Monitor. Watch live behavior for drift and abuse.
  • Refine. Feed real findings back into how values are taught.
  • Trust. Let the world see the character, not just the promise.

14 THE REFLECTION

A character, made visible

Out of seven hundred thousand conversations, a consistent character emerged.

  • Mostly aligned. Broadly helpful, honest, and harmless — as intended.
  • Deeply contextual. Values that arrive when the moment needs them.
  • Not yet finished. Shadows to watch, methods to sharpen, work to continue.

The values weren't declared. They were discovered — living, already, in the wild.
